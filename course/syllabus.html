    Responsibility for Learning.

    Every student in this course is responsible for their own learning; this is especially true given the modality of the course. The instructor will make every effort to support you; however, if you need any help, it is your responsibility to reach out to the instructor.

    Course Content.

    The course will focus on the mathematical foundations for calculus. The course covers exponential and logarithmic functions; trigonometric functions, equations, and identities; and matrices and solving systems of equations and inequalities. Applications to real world situations will be considered throughout.
    🔗
    🔗

    Given the primarily online format of the course, students should be fluent with basic technological tasks such as getting online, using email, and uploading documents. In order to take this course, you need to have access to the following (if any of these are an issue, you should contact your instructor by Friday, July 17.):
    🔗
    • A computer capable of accessing the internet.
      🔗
      🔗
    • A high-speed internet connection.
      🔗
      🔗
    • A phone or tablet (equipped with an appropriate app) with working camera capable of scanning written work into a single PDF, or a scanner with this same capability.
      🔗
      🔗

    Required Course Materials.

    All course documents, materials, and assignments will be posted on MyOpenMath (MOM), a free online course management system specifically designed for mathematics. Course material will be presented through instructor-created videos available on MyOpenMath. A textbook is not required, though we will loosely follow the free open-source textbook "Algebra and Trigonometry 2e" by Abramson; links to both an online version and a downloadable PDF will be posted on MyOpenMath.

    Calculator Usage.

    A scientific, non-graphing calculator capable of computing logarithmic and trigonometric values is required, though its use will be limited to only a handful of sections. I recommend the TI-30X IIS, which you can usually find for around $15. You will be asked to complete most quiz/exam questions without use of a calculator. Graphing calculators, phones, and tablets are never allowed!

    Subsection Course Structure, Assignments, and Grading

    Course Structure.

    This is a blended asynchronous course which combines asynchronous online work and in-person exams. All direct learning of course material will be done independently using a series of instructor-created videos. This allows you to work on your own schedule and at your own pace throughout the summer session (while meeting deadlines) with the ability to go back and review material as needed. It is strongly encouraged you try to work on course material each day throughout the week. The course is broken up into 10 units, due on most Tuesdays and Fridays throughout the session; each unit is roughly equivalent to a full week of a normal semester. The typical unit will cover two major topics, and will consist of the following:
    🔗
    • A series of videos posted on MyOpenMath presenting the course content.
      🔗
      🔗
    • Two problem sets, one for each major topic, completed on MyOpenMath.
      🔗
      🔗
    • A timed closed-note online quiz administered on MyOpenMath will follow the even-numbered units.
      🔗
      🔗
    More details about these assessments can be found below in the appropriate sections; a schedule with due dates can be found at the end of the syllabus. Due to the amount of work required for the course, you are strongly encouraged to work on course material each day throughout the week, as much as possible. This will lead to a better understanding of the course material, and subsequently a better course grade. Despite the asynchronous nature of the course, you are welcome to reach out to the instructor at any time with questions. The asynchronicity is designed to provide flexibility on when you work on the course material, not to create a hindrance to your success.
    🔗
    🔗

    Deadlines and LatePasses.

    All assignments in the course will have strict deadlines that must be met to receive credit. To account for any unexpected emergencies or technical issues that may occur, we will utilize the LatePass feature in MyOpenMath. A LatePass can be redeemed for an automatic 48-hour extension on an assignment (some exclusions apply, listed below); to use a LatePass, click the ’Use LatePass’ button next to the appropriate assignment on MyOpenMath and the platform will automatically extend the due date for that assignment to 48 hours after the original due date. Each student will be given 12 LatePasses to use as they wish throughout the course, with the caveat that only one LatePass can be used on any single assignment. LatePasses cannot be used on an online homework set that you view in ’Practice’ mode prior to redeeming the LatePass. If circumstances require a more substantial extension, contact your instructor as soon as possible to discuss this possibility; documentation of the circumstances will be required.
    🔗
    🔗

    Content Videos.

    All course content will be presented using a series of instructor-created videos, posted to YouTube and available through MyOpenMath. The videos will provide all relevant definitions, technique descriptions, and completely worked-out examples. The videos in each unit will typically have a combined run time of around an hour (though this might vary a bit from unit to unit); most individual videos will be 10-12 minutes in length. You should take notes on the videos, just as you would in a lecture-based course, and use these notes when completing the problem sets and preparing for quizzes and exams.
    🔗
    🔗

    Problem Sets.

    There will be two problem sets for each unit, completed in MyOpenMath. Problem sets are due by 11:59pm on Tuesdays and Fridays, according to the schedule at the end of this syllabus, and are eligible for LatePasses when not opened in ’Practice’ mode. Your score on each set will be considered as a percent and each set will be weighted equally when computing your problem set grade. No problem set scores will be dropped. The problem sets are set up so you can try most problems two times, then the correct answer will be shown. If you miss a problem and want to improve your score, you can click the ’Try a similar problem’ link, which will give you a new question of the same type. You can keep on working on versions of a question until you get it correct. Each problem set will contain a few problems in which you will need to submit a worked out solution to a problem; these problems will have only one attempt and answers will not be available until after the due date has passed. You must earn at least 50% on an assignment for your score to count towards your grade; any set on which you earn less than 50% will be counted as a 0. Assignments will remain available in ’Practice’ mode after their due date to provide additional practice of concepts; new versions of problems can be generated and answered, but scores will not be saved. Be aware that opening an online homework assignment in ’Practice’ mode will disqualify you from being able to redeem a LatePass on that assignment!
    🔗
    Although you will be entering only answers, I encourage you to work out each problem carefully on paper. This will help you follow your reasoning when you are reviewing for quizzes and exams. I strongly encourage you to start each problem set right away so you will have ample time to ask questions. The exercises in the problem sets are intended to both provide practice of concepts covered in the videos as well as extend these concepts beyond what is done in the videos - in particular, you should expect to find some of the exercises difficult. Many quiz and exam problems will be based on the problem set exercises, including the more difficult ones, so you should aim to understand the underlying idea for each assigned exercise. You should expect to spend several hours each week working on the problem sets.
    🔗
    If you are having trouble on a problem, you may use the ’Message Instructor’ link, which will allow you to send me a message through MyOpenMath; the specific problem you are working on will be automatically included in the message. I can then reply to the message with some guidance. These messages are internal to MyOpenMath and not email, so my reply will go to your messages when you log in to MyOpenMath.
    🔗
    🔗

    Online Quizzes.

    A timed, closed-note quiz in MyOpenMath will follow the even-numbered units, and will be due by 11:59pm on the following Tuesday/Friday along with the next unit; the quiz following Unit 10 will be due on Sunday, August 23 (the day before the final exam). All quiz scores will be weighted equally; none will be dropped. You will have 60 minutes to take each quiz; once you start, you must finish within 60 minutes. Once you submit your quiz in MyOpenMath, you must take pictures of or scan all relevant work you performed and submit it on MyOpenMath. You must submit all work which supports your quiz answers within 20 minutes of completing your quiz; failure to do so will result in a score of 0 on that quiz. I reserve the right to deduct points from a quiz problem if it is not fully supported in your submitted work, even if the answer is correct. Although quizzes will be completed online and will not be monitored, quizzes are intended to be closed-note; any evidence of cheating will result in a 0 on that quiz.

    Subsubsection Course Policies

    Extra Credit.

    There will be no extra credit available in this course. If you are interested in raising your grade, take the time you would have spent on an extra credit assignment and spend it making sure you get more "regular credit" instead: master a topic you’ve found difficult, find exercises in the textbook to work on, spend a few extra hours studying, etc.
    🔗
    🔗

    Studying and Coursework.

    To succeed in this course, you will need to dedicate a significant amount of time to working on course material. Mathematics is learned by doing, meaning it is important to work numerous problems to fully understand the concepts we cover. The assigned problem sets is the minimum amount you should be completing; if you feel that you don’t fully understand a concept, work extra problems from a textbook or ask me for some additional practice problems. This is a 4-credit summer course, which means there should be 8 learning hours per week (a fully face-to-face section of the course would be required to meet 8 hours per week). A general rule in all college courses is for each hour of class per week you spend a minimum of two hours outside of class on homework, studying, reading the text, etc. Since this course has 8 online learning hours per week, you should plan to spend an additional 16 hours (for a total of at least 24 hours) working on math per week. This will, of course, vary for every student.
    🔗
    🔗

    Accommodations.

    If you anticipate issues related to the format or requirements of this course, please contact me as early as possible to discuss ways to ensure your full participation. If you believe formal, disability-related accommodations are appropriate, it is very important you register with the Student Accessibility Services office and notify me of your eligibility for such accommodations. We can then plan how to best coordinate your accommodations.

    Academic Integrity and AI Usage.

    The WSU Mathematics Department does not tolerate academic dishonesty! For an explanation of what is considered "academic dishonesty" at WSU, please see the policy included in the 2025-2026 Undergraduate Catalog. All instructors in the WSU Mathematics Department are required to report all incidents of academic dishonesty observed, detected, or otherwise determined to have occurred to the WSU Academic Central File. WSU requires some penalty be imposed for every reported violation of the academic honesty policy. All incidents of academic dishonesty in this course will result in (at least) a score of 0 on that assignment; additional penalties will be imposed at the instructor’s discretion and/or as required by the Mathematics Department Academic Honesty Policy. In this course, instances of academic dishonesty will include (but are not limited to):
    🔗
    • Submitting any assignment you did not complete yourself, either work produced by a classmate/friend or from an online resource (such as an online calculator) or third party (such as Chegg, Course Hero, etc.).
      🔗
      🔗
    • Using course materials/notes, the internet, or a calculator/other device in any unauthorized way during an online quiz or an in-person exam.
      🔗
      🔗
    • Sharing the contents of a quiz with a classmate.
      🔗
      🔗
    Generative artificial intelligence (AI), such as ChatGPT, Gemini, Copilot, etc., should not be used in any way to complete graded assignments in this course. Any evidence of generative AI use on an assignment will be considered an academic honesty violation and will result in at least a 0 on that assignment.

    Subsection Student Learning Outcomes and LASC

    Student Learning Outcomes.

    After successfully completing this course, students will be able to ...
    🔗
    1. Justify their mathematical reasoning and use correct mathematical notation and terminology.
      🔗
      🔗
    2. Solve exponential, logarithmic, and trigonometric equations, as well as systems of linear equations.
      🔗
      🔗
    3. Analyze and transform graphs of exponential, logarithmic, and trigonometric functions.
      🔗
      🔗
    4. Simplify and evaluate expressions involving logarithms, trigonometric functions, and inverse trigonometric functions.
      🔗
      🔗
    5. Prove and apply trigonometric identities, including those involving the sums/differences of angles, half angles, and double angles.
      🔗
      🔗
    6. Solve problems from real life applications such as growth and decay, triangle measurement, or harmonic motion.
      🔗
      🔗
